Binding

Whether we have just a few sheets of printed braille, hundreds, or even thousands, you may want to bind the material together for convenience.

If you intend binding your braille sheets, allowances will need to be made to avoid printing where you intend to bind.

You will need to know how much room to leave for binding before going on to Global Setup. Remember too that this may have a bearing on how many characters can be printed on each sheet of paper.

Single or Double Sided:

Terms vary, but generally speaking, if you are binding single sided braille, the unprinted space on the left hand side is referred to as the "binding margin."

If you are printing on both sides of the page, the space left for binding is called the "gutter." In DBT, the space left for binding is called a "Binding Margin"

regardless of whether the document is single- or double-sided.

Individual circumstances vary according to cost, local requirements, etc. Here we briefly describe some of the more popular methods of binding.

Staple (Side or Corner):

Stapling is the cheapest and simplest binding method. Sheets can either be stapled in the top left corner, or down the left hand side. This is OK for 2 - 10 sheets.

Staple (Magazine Style):

Some embossers (printers) will print 4 pages at a time onto extra wide paper. For example, with a 12 page document, the embosser will print pages 1, 2, 11 & 12, then 3, 4, 9 & 10, and finally 5, 6, 7 & 8. A heavy duty stapler is used to place a staple in the "centerfold", after which the document is folded. Normally a maximum of 40-50 sheets can be stapled this way.

Treasury Tagging:

This method utilizes the sprocket holes at the edge of the paper to keep sheets together.

Ring Binding:

2, 3 or 4 holes are pre-punched in the paper. After the sprocket holed section has been removed, sheets can then be filed in normal commercial ring binders.

Punching can also be done manually.

Comb Binding:

Paper is pre-punched with oblong holes down one side. After removal of the sprocket holed section, sheets can be bound using plastic "combs."

Comb binder punching can also be done manually.

Wiro Binding:

"Wiro" is a popular proprietary form of binding, especially in braille library environments.

Saddle Stitch:

This is similar to magazine style stapling, but here strong thread is used instead of staples.

Perfect Binding:

This method is similar to that used for binding paperback books. However, it does not lend itself easily to binding braille pages.
