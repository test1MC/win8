Using Perky Duck

 

Perky Duck is designed to work in the same way the Perkins Brailler braille typewriter works by using six keys on your keyboard and the space bar. It is designed for use by students learning to type braille by hand for the purpose of certification in braille transcription.

Typing a Braille Character

 

The braille cell consists of six dots: three on the left which are dots 1, 2 and 3 (starting from the top) and three on the right which are dots 4, 5 and 6 (starting from the top). See the topic The Basics of Braille. You can type any braille cell configuration in Perky Duck by simultaneously typing keys on your keyboard to correspond to the dots you want to appear in the cell. The keys you would normally use are F (dot 1), D (dot 2), S (dot 3), J (dot 4), K (dot 5) and L (dot 6). The space bar skips a cell and leaves a blank space.

For example if you want to type a braille letter A, which is a dot 1 with the other five dots left blank, you would type the F key. To create a “for” sign, which is all six dots, you would type the F, D, S, J, K and L keys all at once.  See Troubleshooting if you find that dots are frequently missing from braille cells.

Formatting in Perky Duck

 

Like a braille typewriter, Perky Duck does not have any automated formatting features, such as the ability to tab, center words or right justify text. These tasks are accomplished manually. Your braille instructor will explain how to do this.

Perky Duck does have a word wrap feature and an adjustable warning bell to tell you when you are nearing the end of a line.
